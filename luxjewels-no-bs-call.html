How Much Should You Spend on an Engagement Ring?

Whatever number is in your head right now, that number came from De Beers advertising. Not financial advisors. Not wedding traditions. Not a study. The "one month salary" rule launched in 1938. It moved to "two months" in the 1980s. By the 1990s, De Beers was pushing "three months" in some markets just to see how far they could push spending. There is no financial logic behind any of those numbers.

How to Choose an Engagement Ring

People open 14 browser tabs, compare 4Cs spreadsheets, read conflicting blog posts, and end up more confused than when they started. Here is the order that actually works.

01

Know Their Style Before You Shop a Single Stone

Before you look at one diamond, study what she already wears. Does she wear gold or silver? Bold pieces or delicate ones? Vintage looks or modern? Does she work with her hands, or would a high-set stone catch on things?

If you do not know, look at her Instagram saves. Check her Pinterest. Ask her sister or best friend. Getting this right saves you from buying the wrong ring in the right price range.

02

Set Your Priority Among the 4Cs

You cannot max out carat, color, clarity, and cut in any realistic budget. Something gives. Decide what matters most before you shop:

  • Cut matters most, always. Pick Excellent cut. Never compromise here. Cut is why a stone sparkles.
  • If carat size matters most, drop color to H or I and clarity to SI1. Eye-clean SI1 looks the same as VS2 to the naked eye.
  • If color matters most, stay D to F and accept a smaller carat weight.
  • If clarity matters most, go VS1 or higher. Most buyers do not need this.

Most buyers should rank: Cut, Carat, Color, Clarity in that order. Lab grown stones let you move higher on every scale within the same budget.

03

Shape and Setting

Match shape to her style, not to what you think engagement rings should look like. Round brilliants cost the most per carat. Oval elongates the finger and has been the fastest-growing shape since 2022. Emerald cut needs high clarity because the step-cut reveals every inclusion. Princess cut gives a modern geometric look at a better price per carat than round brilliant.

Solitaire is the simplest and most affordable. Halo adds sparkle and makes the center stone look 15 to 20 percent larger. Three-stone carries meaning: past, present, future.

04

Lab Grown or Mined

This is the last decision, and it is the one most couples get wrong by deciding first. Lab grown diamonds are chemically identical to mined (the FTC confirmed that in 2018), cost 70 to 80 percent less, and look the same to the naked eye. Mined diamonds hold resale value better, running 25 to 50 percent of retail versus 5 to 20 percent for lab grown.

I was the first jeweler in Canada to offer lab grown diamonds in 2015. I also still sell mined diamonds. I do not push one over the other. On the call we figure out which one fits your situation.

Common Diamond Buying Scams

I have reviewed hundreds of quotes from other sellers on the No-BS Call over the years. The same five scams keep showing up. Most are not illegal. They are just "standard industry practice" that costs buyers thousands. Here is how each one works and how to spot it before you hand over your money.

Scam 01

The Bluff Diamond

Someone hands you a certificate that reads F color, VS2 clarity, Excellent cut. Sounds beautiful. But the stone has a grey undertone in daylight, visible inclusions near the table, and noticeable asymmetry. The certificate matches a better stone than what you are actually buying.

How it works: the seller used a certification lab that grades loosely. EGL, WGI, or a no-name lab. GIA would call that stone H or SI1. The weaker lab called it F/VS2. You paid F/VS2 money. You got H/SI1 quality. That is a bluff diamond.

How to spot it: only accept GIA or IGI certification. Nothing else. If the seller cannot or will not provide either, assume there is a reason.
Scam 02

Pressure Tactics and Fake Deadlines

"This price is good today only." "Someone else is looking at this stone." "The holiday sale ends at midnight." Diamond pricing does not expire on your decision timeline. Real discounts are documented, time-boxed, and apply to categories or site-wide, not to individual stones based on your hesitation.

If a salesperson is pressuring you to decide right now, walk away. The same stone, the same quality, the same specs exist elsewhere without the pressure.

How to spot it: pressure means they are worried you will discover something if you take time to think.
Scam 03

Inflated Appraisals

You buy a ring for $8,000. The jeweler hands you an appraisal saying it is worth $16,000. You feel like you got a deal. Here is what happened: that appraisal is not a fair market value. It is a marketing tool. It exists so you feel good about the purchase and so insurance premiums are higher.

Real resale value on engagement rings runs 25 to 50 percent of retail for mined, and 5 to 20 percent for lab grown, regardless of the appraisal number.

How to protect yourself: get your appraisal from an independent GIA-certified appraiser who does not sell jewelry. Never use the retailer's own appraisal as a market-value benchmark.
Scam 04

Lab Diamond Mislabeling

Some sellers present lab grown diamonds without disclosing they are lab grown, or present mined diamonds as lab grown to justify lower prices. Both happen. Both are problems.

How to protect yourself: ask in writing, "Is this diamond laboratory grown or mined?" Check the certificate. GIA and IGI both explicitly state "Laboratory Grown" or "Natural" at the top. If the seller gets evasive or changes the subject, walk.
Scam 05

Fake Retail Prices with Fake Discounts

Ring listed at $10,000 retail, sold "today" for $6,000. Forty percent off. Sounds great. Usually that $10,000 retail price is fiction. The ring was never sold at $10,000. Nobody ever paid that. The "discount" price is the actual price.

How to spot it: pull up the same stone specs at three other retailers. If the "discount" price matches what others charge at full price, you are paying market rate, not getting a deal. If the price is noticeably lower than comparable stones elsewhere, look harder. It is usually a quality difference, not a bargain.

Why Engagement Rings Cost What They Do

Some diamonds are marked up way too high. Not because they are rare. Because of how many hands touch them between the ground and your finger.

Why Traditional Retail Markups Are That High

Traditional jewelry stores need 200 to 300 percent markup over wholesale just to survive. Rent on a Yaletown or Rodeo Drive showroom. Sales staff commissions. Inventory sitting in a case waiting for someone to walk in. Marketing and advertising. Loss prevention. All of that comes out of your ring price.

Online retailers operate at 30 to 75 percent markup. That is why online pricing is more affordable. They do not have a physical store, commission-based salespeople, or a window display filled with unsold inventory.

Custom jewelers like Lux Jewels work differently. We do not carry inventory. We do not have a showroom. Custom work is priced by labor and materials instead of retail markup. The same-looking ring from a chain retail store runs significantly more. Same visual. Different math.
